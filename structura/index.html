Close to a city, we can only see the brightest stars in the night sky. This view shows a large fraction of sky above your head. Notice the size of the Moon.

Away from cities, we can see many more stars by eye. In remote locations one can see up to two thousand stars. Our ancestors imagined lines between some of these stars to create constellations and tell stories about them.

For many of these stars, the light took thousands of years to reach us.

If our eyes were bigger or more sensitive, one could see even more stars. With telescopes, millions of stars can be seen. All these stars reside in a gigantic system. This is our galaxy, the Milky Way. For many of these stars, the light took a million years to reach us.

Looking beyond all the stars, we find an incredible number of galaxies. Each dot in this image is an entire galaxy, made of a billion stars like our own Milky Way. These galaxies have different shapes. Many of them have a spiral structure and are made of young, blue stars. They are distributed along gigantic filaments.

The light from these galaxies traveled for tens of millions of years to reach us.

xxxx

At the intersection of these filaments, we find clusters of galaxies, similar to large cities found at the intersection of highways. The one seen close to the center of the image is called the Coma Cluster. It contains thousands of galaxies.

At greater distances, it becomes harder to see the blue spiral galaxies. One tends to see only the bright, elliptical galaxies. They are made of old, yellow stars. As we look further away, the filaments and empty regions look smaller, like everything seen from far away.

The light from these elliptical galaxies traveled for a billion years to reach us.

As the Universe expands, it makes the distant objects look redder. The bright elliptical galaxies now appear red. They are ten times bigger than typical spiral galaxies.

The light from these elliptical galaxies traveled for five billion years to reach us.

At even greater distances, normal galaxies made of stars are too faint to be seen. However, we can still observe some bright blue points. This is light emitted by very hot gas falling onto supermassive black holes which live at the center of certain galaxies.

The light from these distant objects has traveld about half the age of the universe.

Looking even further away, the expansion of the Universe is again apparent and makes these bright points appear orange and red.

At such distances, we are seeing the Universe when it was “young”. Only 1 billion years old.

When we reach these distances, the Universe looks dark. It is opaque due to a high concentration of hydrogen in space. The photons visible to our eyes cannot propagate through this medium. As a result, the Universe appears dark to us. Only infrared and radio waves can emerge from this region. During this period, the first stars are being formed in this blanket of gas.

At these distances, the Universe is only a few hundred million years old.

Looking even further, we observe the first flash of light which was emitted everywhere in the Universe. In contrast to the rest of the movie, this (real) picture was not taken by a standard camera but by a special instrument which can see microwaves. These waves are similar to those existing inside a microwave oven.

They traveled for more than 13 billion years to reach us today. They carry the imprint of the initial conditions of the Universe we live in.
